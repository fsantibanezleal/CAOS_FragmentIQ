\begin{abstract}
How well a blast fragmented the rock, the size distribution of the muckpile, sets the energy cost of every
crushing and grinding stage downstream, and it is routinely estimated by photographing the muckpile and analysing
the image. Such image-based granulometry is convenient and its biases are well known but rarely quantified against
ground truth, because on a real muckpile the true size distribution is unknown. FragmentIQ closes that loop on
synthetic muckpiles whose true fragmentation is known by construction: it runs the classical watershed
delineation and Rosin-Rammler fit of the standard tools, and measures the error. The result is a clean,
quantified statement of the two biases. First, the watershed \emph{over-segments}: it finds one-and-a-half to more
than two times as many fragments as truly exist, splitting single blocks into pieces, and as a consequence the
recovered $P_{50}$ and $P_{80}$ fall systematically below the truth, by roughly twenty to thirty percent across
seven cases. Second, and correctable, a size calibration that accounts for the missed fines and the
over-segmentation reduces the median $P_{50}$ error from $28.4\%$ to $3.98\%$, and a learned convolutional
edge-delineation, in place of the classical watershed, trims it modestly further (from $27.2\%$ to $23.8\%$ with a
boundary F1 of $0.997$). The honest lesson is that a raw image-based fragmentation number is biased fine by tens
of percent and should be calibrated before it is trusted, and the tool makes that bias and its correction visible
and reproducible; every number and figure regenerates from the committed artifacts.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\IEEEPARstart{T}{he} degree to which a drill-and-blast round fragments the rock, quantified as the particle-size
distribution of the resulting muckpile, is one of the most consequential numbers in a mine: a finer muckpile is
cheaper to dig, haul and, above all, crush and grind, so blast design is tuned to it, and predicting it from blast
parameters is the classic Kuz-Ram problem~\cite{kuznetsov,cunningham}. Measuring it after the fact, by sieving, is
impractical at production scale, so the standard practice is image-based granulometry: photograph the muckpile,
delineate the visible fragments, convert their areas to sizes, and fit a Rosin-Rammler distribution~\cite{rosin}.
The commercial tools that do this (WipFrag, Split) are built on a watershed
segmentation of the image~\cite{maerz,vincent}, and their biases, over-segmentation of large blocks, blindness to
fines below the image resolution, are well documented in the literature and usually acknowledged with a warning
rather than a number, because on a real muckpile the true distribution against which to measure the error is not
known.

FragmentIQ measures the error, by running the standard pipeline on synthetic muckpiles whose true fragmentation
is known by construction. It performs the same watershed delineation and Rosin-Rammler fit as the classical
tools, reports the recovered $P_{10}$, $P_{50}$ and $P_{80}$, and compares them to the ground truth over seven
cases spanning coarse to fine and monosized to broad. On that footing it quantifies the two biases and shows that
one of them is largely correctable, and it tests whether a learned edge-delineation, in place of the classical
watershed, helps. The contribution is not a new fragmentation algorithm; it is the honest, ground-truthed
quantification of the bias of the standard one, and the demonstration that a size calibration removes most of it.

\section{The Systematic Bias}\label{sec:bias}

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.99\columnwidth]{fig-bias.pdf}
\caption{The bias of image-based fragmentation analysis, measured against known ground truth over seven synthetic
muckpiles. \textbf{(a)} Recovered versus true $P_{50}$ and $P_{80}$: every point falls below the line of perfect
recovery, so the watershed pipeline reports sizes systematically finer than truth, by roughly $20$--$30\%$, and the
gap widens for the coarse tail. \textbf{(b)} The cause: fragments found versus true fragment count, every point
above the one-to-one line, the watershed \emph{over-segments}, finding $1.5$ to more than $2$ times as many
fragments as exist by splitting single blocks, which shifts the size distribution fine.}
\label{fig:bias}
\end{figure}

The core result is Fig.~\ref{fig:bias}, and it is a clean confirmation of what the literature warns of, now with
numbers. On every one of the seven synthetic muckpiles, the recovered $P_{50}$ and $P_{80}$ fall below the true
values (Fig.~\ref{fig:bias}a): the pipeline reports a muckpile finer than it really is, by roughly twenty to
thirty percent, and the under-recovery is worst for the coarse cases (a true $P_{80}$ of $1096$~mm is recovered as
$794$). The mechanism is visible in Fig.~\ref{fig:bias}b: the watershed \emph{over-segments}, finding between one
and a half and more than two times as many fragments as truly exist, because it splits single large blocks along
internal texture and shadow into several apparent fragments. More, smaller apparent fragments drag the size
distribution fine, which is exactly the direction of the observed bias. This is not a coding error, it is the
intrinsic behaviour of watershed delineation on rock imagery, and quantifying it, rather than warning about it in
the abstract, is the point: a raw image-based $P_{50}$ should be read as an underestimate of tens of percent.

\section{Correcting the Bias}\label{sec:correction}

\begin{figure}[!t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=0.99\columnwidth]{fig-correction.pdf}
\caption{Correcting the bias. \textbf{(a)} A size calibration that accounts for the over-segmentation and the
missed fines reduces the median $P_{50}$ error from $28.4\%$ (raw watershed) to $3.98\%$, most of the bias is
systematic and therefore correctable. \textbf{(b)} Replacing the classical watershed with a learned convolutional
edge-delineation trims the error modestly, from $27.2\%$ to $23.8\%$, at a boundary F1 of $0.997$; better edges
help, but the dominant fix is the size calibration, not the delineation.}
\label{fig:correction}
\end{figure}

The bias being systematic, it is largely correctable (Fig.~\ref{fig:correction}). A size calibration, fit on a
disjoint tuning set and accounting for both the over-segmentation and the fines that fall below the image
resolution, reduces the median $P_{50}$ error from $28.4\%$ for the raw watershed to $3.98\%$
(Fig.~\ref{fig:correction}a), an order-of-magnitude reduction, because the bias is a consistent fine-shift that a
single calibration can undo. The second lever, a better delineator, helps less. Replacing the classical watershed
with a learned convolutional edge-detector (achieving a boundary F1 of $0.997$ against the true fragment outlines)
lowers the raw $P_{50}$ error from $27.2\%$ to $23.8\%$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:correction}b), a real but modest gain: the
learned edges do split fewer blocks spuriously, but they do not, on their own, close the gap the way the
calibration does. The honest ranking of fixes matters for practice: invest in calibrating the size estimate
against a known reference, and treat a better segmentation as a secondary improvement, not the cure.

\section{Discussion and Scope}\label{sec:discuss}

The report's value is a number where the field usually has a caveat. Image-based fragmentation analysis is biased
fine, here by twenty to thirty percent at $P_{50}$ and $P_{80}$, driven by watershed over-segmentation, and that
bias is systematic enough that a size calibration removes almost all of it, taking the $P_{50}$ error to about
four percent, while a learned segmentation helps only modestly. A practitioner should take from this that a raw
muckpile image number is an underestimate to be calibrated, not a measurement to be trusted, and that the
calibration, not the segmentation method, is where accuracy is won. The scope is stated plainly. The seven cases
are synthetic muckpiles, used precisely because their true size distribution is known and the bias can therefore
be measured, which is not possible on a real muckpile without independent sieving; the magnitudes here are
representative of the mechanism, not a calibration transferable to a specific site, which would need its own
ground-truthed reference. The tool has no photo upload in this build; the cases are the inputs, and it is a
didactic and diagnostic instrument, not a production granulometry system. None of this is hidden; the tool shows
the recovered curve, the fit and the caveats on every case.

\section{Conclusion}

Measured against ground truth on synthetic muckpiles, image-based fragmentation analysis by watershed delineation
is biased fine: it over-segments, finding up to twice the true fragment count, and recovers $P_{50}$ and $P_{80}$
some twenty to thirty percent below truth. The bias is systematic, so a size calibration reduces the median
$P_{50}$ error from $28.4\%$ to $3.98\%$, while a learned edge-delineation helps only modestly ($27.2\%$ to
$23.8\%$). Quantifying the bias where the literature warns of it, and showing that calibration, not segmentation,
is the dominant fix, is the report's contribution, on a reproducible tool that makes the bias and its correction
visible.

\section{Reproduction}\label{app:repro}

The committed artifacts under \texttt{data/derived/} carry the seven cases' true and recovered size distributions
and fragment counts (\texttt{case-results.json}) and the learned-model metrics (\texttt{fq-learned.json}: the raw
and size-corrected $P_{50}$ errors, and the classical versus learned edge-delineation errors with the boundary
F1). The figure scripts under \texttt{manuscripts/fragmentation/figures/} read those artifacts. The synthetic
generator, watershed and fit are deterministic given a case seed, so re-running reproduces
Figs.~\ref{fig:bias}--\ref{fig:correction}.
